\section{Challenges in Bremsstrahlung Imaging}

As introduced in Section~\ref{sec:y90}, unlike diagnostic radionuclides such as \isotope[99m]{Tc} that emit gamma photons at discrete photopeaks, \isotope[90]{Y} has no practically useful prompt gamma emission for SPECT. Imaging therefore relies on detecting the continuous spectrum of Bremsstrahlung photons generated as emitted $\beta$-particles decelerate in tissue \cite{woerner_advances_2022}. This emission physics gives rise to several fundamental challenges for quantitative reconstruction:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Continuous energy spectrum:} The emitted photon energies span a continuum from (near) zero up to the maximum $\beta$-energy of 2.28~MeV \cite{be_table_2004} (see Figure~\ref{fig:brems_spectra}). In conventional SPECT, scatter may be estimated using energy windows adjacent to a photopeak; for \isotope[90]{Y}, the absence of a photopeak makes it difficult to separate primary photons from those that have undergone Compton scatter within the patient. As a result, more sophisticated scatter-modelling and correction strategies are typically required \cite{rong_development_2012}.
    
    \item \textbf{Septal penetration and collimator design:} The high-energy tail of the Bremsstrahlung spectrum increases the probability of septal penetration, which degrades spatial resolution and contrast. Collimator choice and optimisation are therefore critical to limit penetration while maintaining adequate sensitivity \cite{rong_collimator_2013}.
    
    \item \textbf{Bremsstrahlung-induced blur:} Bremsstrahlung photons are produced along the electron track rather than at a single point, so the effective photon emission location is distributed relative to the \isotope[90]{Y} decay site. This introduces an additional range-dependent blurring component that is not present in photopeak imaging and can further degrade reconstructed resolution \cite{rong_development_2012} (see Figure.
\end{itemize}

\section{Optimisation Strategies and Monte Carlo Modeling}

To overcome these limitations, optimisation must occur at both the acquisition and reconstruction stages. While hardware choices, such as the preference for Medium Energy General Purpose (MEGP) or High Energy General Purpose (HEGP) collimators, can mitigate septal penetration \cite{taherparvar_evaluation_2019, kim_effects_2019, ince_collimator_2021}, substantial gains in quantitative accuracy are achieved through advanced computational modeling \cite{dewaraja_improved_2017}.

Monte Carlo (MC) simulation codes, such as SIMIND \cite{ljungberg_-_2012} and GATE (Geant4 Application for Tomographic Emission) \cite{jan_gate_2004, rault_fast_2010}, play a pivotal role in this optimisation. These tools allow for the generation of accurate scatter kernels, as well as other effects that are specific to the continuous \isotope[90]{Y} spectrum. Some methods have integrated modelling of the spatial offset between the $\beta$-decay location and the point of Bremsstrahlung emission—into Monte Carlo forward projectors to refine the system matrix \cite{rong_development_2012}.

Furthermore, novel reconstruction techniques such as Joint Spectral Reconstruction (JSR) have been proposed to exploit the information contained across multiple energy windows, rather than relying on a single wide integration window \cite{chun_algorithms_2019}. By combining multi-band acquisition with accurate Monte Carlo-based forward modeling, it is possible to improve the precision of activity estimation and contrast recovery in post-SIRT dosimetry \textcolor{red}{Can I fit this in somehow? \section{Additive Corrections}
\label{sec:additive_corrections}

A key difficulty in quantitative \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung SPECT is that a substantial fraction of the measured counts arise from photons that are not well modelled by a purely ``primary-only'' forward projector. In this work we treat these contributions as \emph{additive terms} in the Poisson measurement model, with a particular focus on (patient) scatter.

\subsection{Statistical model with an additive background term}

Let $y \in \mathbb{N}^{M}$ denote the measured projection data (all bins across all views) and let $x \in \mathbb{R}_{+}^{N}$ denote the unknown activity image. We use the standard Poisson model
\begin{equation}
y_i \sim \mathrm{Poisson}\!\left( (A x)_i + b_i \right), \qquad i = 1,\dots,M,
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the system matrix modelling the (primary) detection process (including attenuation and detector response as implemented in our reconstruction pipeline), and $b \in \mathbb{R}_{+}^{M}$ is an \emph{additive} component. In Bremsstrahlung SPECT, $b$ is typically dominated by Compton-scattered photons, with additional contributions possible from other backgrounds (e.g.\ stray radiation or modelling mismatch). The role of additive correction is therefore to provide an estimate of $b$ that is consistent with the acquisition protocol and reconstruction model.

Given a \emph{fixed} $b$, ordered-subsets expectation maximisation (OSEM) yields an approximate maximum-likelihood estimate of $x$ under this Poisson model. However, in \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung imaging, $b$ is large and difficult to estimate from energy-window tricks, motivating model-based strategies.

\subsection{Initial scatter estimate via a pre-trained deep network}

To initialise $b$ we use a deep-learning scatter estimator that predicts scatter projections directly from the measured emission projections (and CT-derived attenuation information), following the general approach of training a convolutional neural network to regress Monte-Carlo-derived ``true'' scatter \cite{xiang_deep_2020}.

In our case this initialisation is explicitly heuristic: the available pre-trained network was developed and trained under a different hardware/protocol configuration (scanner, collimation, energy windowing, and number of views) than the Siemens and Mediso datasets used in this thesis \cite{xiang_deep_2020}. In particular, the network was trained on data acquired on a Symbia Intevo SPECT/CT system with high-energy collimation and a 105--195~keV window with 80 projections, whereas our reconstructions were performed on Siemens or Mediso systems with different energy windows and either MEGP (Siemens) or MLEGP (Mediso) collimation. Despite this domain shift, we found that the predicted scatter term fits the measured data sufficiently well to provide a sensible starting point for subsequent model-based refinement (Figure \ref{fig:nema_scatter_est}). 

\subsection{Iterative OSEM--SIMIND refinement loop}

Starting from the deep-learning initialisation $b^{(0)}$, we then refine the additive term using an alternating procedure:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reconstruction step:} perform an OSEM update of $x$ using the current additive estimate $b^{(k)}$. We used 12 subsets and 5 epochs (total 60 subiterations)
    \item \textbf{Regularisation of the intermediate image:} apply an isotropic Gaussian post-smoothing of 5~mm FWHM to the intermediate reconstruction (used only for the subsequent scatter simulation step).
    \item \textbf{Scatter simulation step:} run a SIMIND-based Monte Carlo scatter simulation using the (smoothed) current activity estimate to produce a new scatter estimate $\widehat{b}^{(k+1)}$.
\end{enumerate}
We repeat this OSEM $\rightarrow$ SIMIND $\rightarrow$ OSEM$+$5~mm smoothing $\rightarrow \dots$ loop for 10 outer iterations. The 5~mm smoothing is used to suppress high-frequency noise that would otherwise be amplified by repeatedly re-simulating scatter from noisy intermediate reconstructions.

\subsection{Offline versus online additive correction strategies}

We consider two practical strategies for how the scatter term is used throughout the full reconstruction:

\paragraph{Offline (fixed) additive correction.}
In the \emph{offline} strategy, we compute a scatter estimate once (after the initialisation/refinement loop) and then keep it fixed for the remainder of the reconstruction. Concretely, we set $b \leftarrow b^{(\star)}$ and run OSEM to convergence (or for a fixed number of iterations) with a constant additive term. This approach is typically stable, but may be biased if the fixed scatter estimate is inconsistent with the final activity estimate.

\paragraph{Online (intermittently updated) additive correction.}
In the \emph{online} strategy, we update the scatter estimate during reconstruction. That is, after some number of OSEM iterations, we re-run the SIMIND scatter simulation using the current image estimate and update $b$. This is conceptually aligned with hybrid Monte-Carlo/analytical reconstruction approaches in which the scatter estimate is updated a small number of times until it stabilises \cite{dewaraja_improved_2017,elschot_quantitative_2013}. In practice, the update cadence is a tunable choice: too frequent updates increase computational cost and can introduce instability; too infrequent updates reduce the benefit relative to the offline approach.

\subsection{Full replacement versus damped scatter updates}

For the online strategy, we investigate two update rules.

\paragraph{(1) Full replacement.}
We set
\begin{equation}
b^{(k+1)} \leftarrow \widehat{b}^{(k+1)}.
\end{equation}
Although appealingly direct, we observed that full replacement can be unstable in some cases, with the behaviour varying across phantoms. A practical interpretation is that once $b$ is changed, the likelihood model being optimised by OSEM is also changed; therefore the overall alternating procedure is not guaranteed to behave monotonically. An 

\paragraph{(2) Damped (relaxed) updates.}
To improve stability, we use a convex combination of the old and new scatter estimates:
\begin{equation}
b^{(k+1)} \leftarrow (1-\alpha)\, b^{(k)} + \alpha\, \widehat{b}^{(k+1)},
\qquad \alpha \in (0,1].
\end{equation}
This can be viewed as a relaxation of the fixed-point iteration $b = \widehat{b}(x)$, reducing sensitivity to noise and modelling mismatch at the cost of slower adaptation. In all experiments here we set $\alpha = 0.2$. This value was chosen heuristically; we did not perform a dedicated study to optimise $\alpha$, but empirically it provided a good stability--convergence trade-off across datasets.

\medskip
Overall, these offline/online and replacement/damping variants provide a practical framework for controlling the bias--variance and stability properties of Bremsstrahlung additive correction, while keeping the reconstruction pipeline compatible with Monte Carlo scatter modelling.

\section{Bremsstrahlung Range Blur}
\label{sec:brem_range_blur}

\begin{figure}[hbtp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/Chapter3/Gaussian_bremss_range.png}
    \caption{Example fits of an isotropic Gaussian proxy to the Monte Carlo source-to-emission displacement distribution for \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung in water (clinical energy windows).}
    \label{fig:gaussian_bremss_range}
\end{figure}

In \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung SPECT, detected photons are not emitted at the \isotope[90]{Y} decay site: Bremsstrahlung photons are generated along the $\beta$-particle track, inducing an additional \emph{range blur} that is absent in photopeak imaging. Several Monte Carlo (MC) frameworks can model this effect using detailed, energy-dependent probability density functions (PDFs) for the displacement between the decay location and the photon emission location. However, in practice, most general-purpose reconstruction toolkits are not designed to ingest arbitrary, precomputed displacement PDFs within the forward projector.

Here we adopt a pragmatic approximation: for clinically relevant energy windows and in water-equivalent tissue, the marginal displacement distribution is observed to be close to Gaussian. We therefore model the Bremsstrahlung range blur as an \emph{isotropic image-space Gaussian} whose variance is selected to match MC-derived displacement statistics (Figure~\ref{fig:gaussian_bremss_range}). This is simple to implement (a single 3D Gaussian smoothing) and is compatible with standard projector architectures.

\subsection{Projector point-spread function}
\label{subsec:projector_psf}

We model the collimator--detector response (CDR) by an approximately Gaussian point-spread function (PSF) whose width increases with the source--collimator distance $h$ and depends on energy $E$. In the projector we represent the depth-dependent blur by a spatially variant isotropic Gaussian with standard deviation
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{lin}}(h;E) = c_0(E) + c_1(E)\, h.
\label{eq:sigma_lin_def}
\end{equation}
This linear model approximates the standard quadrature combination of intrinsic detector resolution and geometric collimator resolution,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{true}}(h;E)
= \bigl[\;\sigma_{\mathrm{intr}}(E)^2 + \sigma_{\mathrm{geom}}(h; E)^2\;\bigr]^{1/2},
\label{eq:sigma_true}
\end{equation}
while avoiding repeated evaluation of a square root inside the projector. The coefficients $c_0(E)$ and $c_1(E)$ are obtained by a least-squares fit of $\sigma_{\mathrm{lin}}(h_i;E)$ to $\sigma_{\mathrm{true}}(h_i;E)$ over a clinically relevant distance range ($h \in [2,20]\,\mathrm{cm}$). Writing
\begin{equation}
\bar h = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N h_i,
\qquad
\bar\sigma = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \sigma_{\mathrm{true}}(h_i;E),
\end{equation}
the closed-form solution (assuming $\sum_i(h_i-\bar h)^2>0$) is
\begin{equation}
c_1(E)
=
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (h_i-\bar h)\,(\sigma_{\mathrm{true}}(h_i;E)-\bar\sigma)}
     {\sum_{i=1}^N (h_i-\bar h)^2},
\qquad
c_0(E)
=
\bar\sigma - c_1(E)\,\bar h .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Intrinsic detector spatial response.}
Intrinsic resolution is measured using a standard source \cite{pells_quantitative_2023} and modelled as an isotropic Gaussian,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PSF}_{\mathrm{intr}}(\mathbf r;E)=
\mathcal N\!\left(\mathbf r;\ \mathbf 0,\ \sigma_{\mathrm{intr}}^2(E)\,\mathbf I\right).
\label{eq:intrinsic}
\end{equation}
When intrinsic resolution is reported as $\mathrm{FWHM}_{\mathrm{intr}}(E)$, we convert using
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{intr}}(E) = \frac{\mathrm{FWHM}_{\mathrm{intr}}(E)}{2\sqrt{2\ln 2}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Geometric collimator response with penetration via effective length.}
For a parallel-hole collimator, the (FWHM) geometric resolution at distance $h$ is commonly approximated by
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{FWHM}_{\mathrm{geom}}(h;E)
\approx d_{\mathrm{eff}}
\left(1+\frac{h}{l_{\mathrm{eff}}(E)}\right),
\label{eq:fwhm_geom}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the effective hole diameter and $l_{\mathrm{eff}}(E)$ is an \emph{effective} hole length used to account for septal penetration. We use
\begin{equation}
l_{\mathrm{eff}}(E) \;=\; l \;-\; \frac{2}{\mu_{\mathrm{Pb}}(E)} ,
\qquad
\mu_{\mathrm{Pb}}(E)\ \text{interpolated from NIST XCOM tables},
\label{eq:leff}
\end{equation}
where $l$ is the physical hole length and $\mu_{\mathrm{Pb}}(E)$ is the linear attenuation coefficient of lead at energy $E$ \cite{noauthor_xcom_2009}. Converting from FWHM to standard deviation gives
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{geom}}(h;E)=\frac{\mathrm{FWHM}_{\mathrm{geom}}(h;E)}{2\sqrt{2\ln 2}}.
\label{eq:sigma_geom}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Bremsstrahlung-specific blur}
\label{subsec:brem_specific_blur}

\paragraph{Gaussian proxy for range blur.}
Let $\Delta$ denote the displacement between the \isotope[90]{Y} decay location and the Bremsstrahlung photon emission location. Rather than using a detailed displacement PDF inside the projector, we approximate the induced range blur by an isotropic Gaussian kernel
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{PSF}_{\mathrm{brem}}(\mathbf r;E)
=\mathcal N\!\left(\mathbf r;\ \mathbf 0,\ \sigma_{\mathrm{brem}}^2(E)\,\mathbf I\right).
\label{eq:brem}
\end{equation}
For each clinical energy window, $\sigma_{\mathrm{brem}}(E)$ is obtained from a coarse look-up table (LUT) by fitting a zero-mean Gaussian proxy to MC displacement curves for \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung in water from GATE fast-simulation studies \cite{rault_fast_2010} (Figure~\ref{fig:gaussian_bremss_range}).

\paragraph{Composite energy windows.}
For a set of energy windows $\mathcal S$ combined into a single projection dataset, we collapse the corresponding Gaussian mixture by second-moment matching:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{clin}}^2
=\frac{\sum_{i\in\mathcal S} w_i\,\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i\in\mathcal S} w_i},
\qquad
w_i \propto \text{per-window counts}.
\label{eq:moment_match}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Implementation in image space.}
Because the convolution of isotropic Gaussians is Gaussian, incorporating Bremsstrahlung range blur is equivalent to adding variances. In practice we implement this as an image-space convolution by $\mathrm{PSF}_{\mathrm{brem}}$ (i.e.\ a 3D Gaussian smoothing) applied to the current activity estimate before forward projection. This keeps the forward model compatible with standard projector implementations while capturing the dominant range-broadening effect in water-equivalent tissue.

\subsection{Validation against SIMIND and GATE forward projections}
\label{subsec:brem_blur_validation}

We assessed how well the isotropic Gaussian proxy reproduces the broader displacement PDFs (including angular effects in GATE) by comparing projected point-source responses produced by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item STIR forward projections with geometric CDR modelling only;
    \item STIR forward projections with geometric CDR plus the proposed isotropic Bremsstrahlung range blur;
    \item SIMIND simulations (which use an approximate isotropic Bremsstrahlung emission model); and
    \item GATE simulations (used here as a higher-fidelity reference that includes angular dependence of Bremsstrahlung emission).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Point-source-in-sphere configuration.}
A true \isotope[90]{Y} point source is not physically realisable at clinical activity levels because the $\beta$-particles have a finite range. To approximate a point source for spatial response measurements, we embedded the activity within a small water-equivalent sphere of radius $1.2\,\mathrm{cm}$, consistent with the maximum $\beta$-particle range in soft tissue/water (approximately $11\,\mathrm{mm}$) \cite{pasciak_radioembolization_2014}. For each configuration we measured the detector-plane FWHM at source--collimator distances of $2$, $5$, $10$, $15$, and $20\,\mathrm{cm}$.

\paragraph{Clinical energy windows.}
Comparisons were performed for three representative clinical windows:
\begin{equation}
[38.24,126.16]\,\mathrm{keV},
\qquad
[50.0,150.0]\,\mathrm{keV},
\qquad
[75.0,225.0]\,\mathrm{keV}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
This validation focuses on water-equivalent media, which is appropriate for the liver (the primary application in post-radioembolization imaging). Differences in range and scatter properties in lung or cortical bone are not explicitly modelled here and are not expected to dominate performance for liver-focused dosimetry.

\section{Residual Correction}
\label{sec:residual_correction}

Fu and Qi proposed a residual-correction (RC) strategy to accelerate reconstructions when an \emph{accurate} forward model is too expensive to apply at every iteration \cite{fu_residual_2010}. In their positron-range setting, the accurate system model can be expressed as a fast projector composed with an additional blurring operator. RC then intermittently estimates the modelling discrepancy and folds it into an additive term, allowing subsequent iterations to proceed using the cheaper projector.

In this work we investigate whether RC can be applied analogously to \isotope[90]{Y} Bremsstrahlung SPECT reconstruction. Empirically, we find that RC behaves well for high-count, low-background phantom data, but can become unreliable in low-count clinical-like regimes where regularisation is necessarily strong. We attribute this behaviour to the fact that RC corrects the \emph{forward prediction} but does not, in general, restore a \emph{matched} forward/adjoint pair inside a regularised reconstruction.

\subsection{Residual correction update}
\label{subsec:rc_update}

Let $A_{\star}$ denote the fast (computationally cheap) system matrix used in the iterative updates and let $A_{\mathrm{MC}}$ denote a more accurate (expensive) system model (here, Monte Carlo based). We consider the Poisson model
\begin{equation}
y_i \sim \mathrm{Poisson}\!\left( (A_{\star}x)_i + b_{E,i} \right),
\end{equation}
where $b_E$ is an additive correction term (e.g.\ scatter). At spaced iterations, we estimate the discrepancy between the accurate and fast forward predictions at the current iterate by approximately solving
\begin{equation}
\hat{x} \approx \underset{x\geq0}{\mathrm{arg\,min}}\;\mathcal{O}\!\left(x \mid y\;; b_E, A_{\star}\right),
\end{equation}
and then forming the residual
\begin{equation}
(\Delta A)\hat{x} \;=\; A_{\mathrm{MC}}\hat{x} - A_{\star}\hat{x}.
\label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
This residual is incorporated into the additive term,
\begin{equation}
b_E^{(\mathrm{new})} \;=\; b_E^{(\mathrm{old})} + (\Delta A)\hat{x},
\label{eq:be_update}
\end{equation}
after which reconstruction proceeds again under the updated additive correction. In favourable regimes, this produces forward predictions closer to those of $A_{\mathrm{MC}}$ while retaining the per-iteration cost of $A_{\star}$.

\subsection{Regularised objective with a Relative Difference prior}
\label{subsec:rc_objective_rdp}

In Bremsstrahlung SPECT, low-count data frequently necessitate comparatively strong regularisation. In all RC experiments reported here, we reconstruct using a Relative Difference prior (RDP), i.e.\ we solve a MAP problem of the form
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}
=
\underset{x\ge 0}{\mathrm{arg\,min}}
\left\{
\Phi_{A_{\star}}(x)
+
\beta R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)
\right\},
\label{eq:map_rdp}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_{A_{\star}}(x)$ is the negative Poisson log-likelihood (up to constants),
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{A_{\star}}(x)
=
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\left[
(A_{\star}x)_i + b_{E,i}
-
y_i \log\!\bigl((A_{\star}x)_i + b_{E,i}\bigr)
\right],
\label{eq:poisson_nll}
\end{equation}
and the Relative Difference prior is defined over a neighbourhood system $\mathcal{N}$ by
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)
=
\sum_{(j,k)\in \mathcal{N}}
w_{jk}\;
\frac{(x_j-x_k)^2}{(x_j+x_k)^2+\eta^2}.
\label{eq:rdp_prior}
\end{equation}
Here, $x_j$ and $x_k$ denote neighbouring voxels, $w_{jk}\ge 0$ are neighbourhood weights, and $\eta>0$ is a small smoothing constant that prevents numerical instability when $x_j+x_k$ is small. The hyperparameter $\beta>0$ controls the overall strength of regularisation.

\subsection{Interpretation via matched versus unmatched projectors}
\label{subsec:rc_unmatched}

To understand why RC can be fragile in low-count, strongly regularised settings, it is helpful to view the method through the lens of \emph{matched} versus \emph{unmatched} projector pairs.

\paragraph{Set-up.}
Suppose the accurate forward model can be written as a fast projector composed with an additional blur (or resolution) operator $K$,
\begin{equation}
P := A_{\mathrm{fast}}K,
\qquad
\bar y(x) = Px + r = A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx + r,
\end{equation}
with $r$ an additive background term and Poisson data $y\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(\bar y(x))$.

\paragraph{Maximum likelihood (no regularisation).}
The negative log-likelihood for forward model $P$ has gradient
\begin{equation}
\nabla \Phi_{P}(x) = P^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{Px+r}\right).
\end{equation}
For $P=A_{\mathrm{fast}}K$, the \emph{matched} stationarity condition is
\begin{equation}
K^T A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx+r}\right)=0.
\label{eq:matched_ml}
\end{equation}
If instead one uses the fast backprojector $A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T$ with the same forward model (an \emph{unmatched} pair), the stationarity condition would be
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx+r}\right)=0.
\label{eq:unmatched_ml}
\end{equation}
In the special case where $K^T$ is injective (and numerically well-conditioned on the relevant subspace), \eqref{eq:matched_ml} and \eqref{eq:unmatched_ml} share the same stationary points, since $K^T z=0 \Rightarrow z=0$. This explains why unmatched backprojection can appear benign in some unregularised, high-count regimes.

\paragraph{How RC fits.}
Residual correction aims to make the \emph{forward prediction} behave like the accurate model without applying $K$ at every iteration, i.e.
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx \;\approx\; A_{\mathrm{fast}}x + \mathrm{RC}(x),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{RC}(x)$ approximates $(A_{\mathrm{fast}}K-A_{\mathrm{fast}})x$ (often using lagged/intermittent updates). If the correction is consistent at a limit point $x^\star$, then the ratio term $y/(A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx+r)$ is effectively ``accurate-forward''.
However, the backprojection used by the iterative update typically remains $A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T$, not $(A_{\mathrm{fast}}K)^T = K^T A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T$. In this sense, RC can be interpreted as correcting the forward model while still behaving like an \emph{unmatched} method on the adjoint side.

\paragraph{Why low-count + RDP regularisation is high-risk.}
With an RDP prior, the MAP stationarity condition (ignoring the non-negativity constraint) is
\begin{equation}
P^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{Px+r}\right) + \beta \nabla R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)=0.
\label{eq:map_stationary_general}
\end{equation}
For $P=A_{\mathrm{fast}}K$, the matched condition becomes
\begin{equation}
K^T A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx+r}\right) + \beta \nabla R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)=0,
\label{eq:matched_map}
\end{equation}
whereas an unmatched analogue would be
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{fast}}^T\!\left(1-\frac{y}{A_{\mathrm{fast}}Kx+r}\right) + \beta \nabla R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)=0.
\label{eq:unmatched_map}
\end{equation}
As total counts decrease, the likelihood term becomes flatter and noisier, so the relative influence of $\beta \nabla R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)$ increases even when $\beta$ is held fixed. Unlike the ML case, the factor $K^T$ multiplies only the data-term gradient in \eqref{eq:matched_map} and does not act on $\nabla R_{\mathrm{RDP}}(x)$. Therefore, for $\beta>0$, matched and unmatched fixed points generally differ. In this prior-dominated regime, using an unmatched backprojector while correcting only the forward prediction can substantially alter the balance between data fidelity and regularisation, leading to marked divergence from the solution associated with the fully matched accurate model.

\subsection{Empirical observations}
\label{subsec:rc_empirical}

For high-count, low-background phantom acquisitions (e.g.\ NEMA-style datasets), RC substantially improves agreement with accurate forward projections and remains stable. For low-count Bremsstrahlung acquisitions, however, we observe that RC can become unreliable when combined with strong RDP regularisation. These observations are consistent with the interpretation above: in prior-dominated regimes, mismatched adjoints change the effective weighting of the data term relative to the RDP, and RC does not in general restore a matched forward/adjoint pair.
